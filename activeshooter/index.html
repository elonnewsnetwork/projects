A killer on campus


Breaking down the best way to respond during an active shooter situation at Elon University.

Run - Hide - Fight

During an active shooter situation, law enforcement experts suggest three possible responses: run, hide, fight. Eric Tellefsen, who has been in law enforcement for the last 34 years, helped develop North Carolina’s active shooter response training. Elon News Network worked with Tellefesen to analyze different locations across campus to understand the best response to campus shooting. View the images below for the full 360 experience.

Moseley Student Center

In this simulation, a gunman has entered the Moseley Student Center, a two-story building at the heart of Elon University’s campus. Moseley is a central location for students that includes a dining hall, market, coffee shop, office space, department headquarters, a kitchen and a student hangout.

Carol Grotnes Belk Library

In this simulation, a gunman has entered the Carol Grotnes Belk Library, which since the start of the school year has had more than 100,000 visits. The three-story building includes 43 study rooms and for most days of the week is open 24/7. A 16,000 square-foot extension to the library was opened in Sept. 2018.

LaRose Student Center

In this simulation, a gunman has entered the 100,000-square foot LaRose Student Center. This building is the newest addition to the Historic Neighborhood, which is home to more than 620 students — 96% of them being freshmen. The center opened this September and was built to provide a central gathering space for students, faculty and staff.

McEwen Dining Hall

The most recently renovated dining location includes a dining hall and two retail options: Pei Wei and Village Juice. The two story building finished renovations last year and is Historic Neighborhood’s main food supply.

Phi Beta Kappa Commons

One of Elon University’s most beloved traditions — College Coffee — is hosted in Phi Beta Kappa Commons. The weekly Tuesday event brings together university administrators, faculty, staff and students to celebrate the start of the week with pastries and coffee.

Sankey Hall Classroom

Located next to Clohan Dining Hall, Sankey Hall is the newest extension to the business school. The 30,000-square-foot building is home to facilities such as the Center for Creativity, the Family Professional Sales Center, a Design Thinking Center and Financial Education Center.

As mass shootings continue to plague the country, Elon University prepares for the worse case scenerio

By Anton L. Delgado | Managing Editor | @antonldelgado

Memories from the worst school shooting in U.S. history resurface semesterly in the mind of Cara Lucia — who was a doctoral student at Virginia Tech University when a gunman killed 32 people in 2007.

More than a decade later, Lucia is now teaching sport management at Elon University. At the beginning of each semester, she spends two to three classes teaching her students what to do in an active shooter situation.

“I always tell my students, I’m not trying to make you live from a place of fear. This is about us being aware and protecting ourselves and the people of our community,” Lucia said.

At the start of the academic school year, the university sponsored two active shooter training sessions for faculty. Eric Tellefsen, a law enforcement professional, presented faculty members with the “run, hide, fight” response protocol to an active shooter situation.

“The more times these things have occured, the more we realized that lives can be saved when the individual people that are there know what to do,” Tellefsen said.

If gunshots were fired on campus, the first law enforcement department to respond would be the university’s campus police, which is headquartered in Oaks Neighborhood.

The department employs 12 community service and 21 police officers, which is more than the number of officers in the town of Elon and Gibsonville police departments.

According to Joel Thomas, community liaison sergeant for campus police, there are usually three to five police officers on campus at all times.

One of those officers is Corporal Matt Norriss who received special active shooter training. Since then, Norriss has been advocating for continued training.

On Oct. 25, campus police participated in its second active shooter training of the year — 25 local law enforcement and emergency service departments participated in the simulation, which was held in Alamance Community College.

During the simulation and in the event of a real crisis, Debbie Hatfield, an emergency management coordinator for Alamance County, coordinates the actions of different first responder departments.

In the event of a school shooting at Elon University, every law enforcement and emergency service department in the surrounding counties would be radioed in for assistance.

According to Hatfield, these interagency trainings and simulations will help save lives during a real emergency.
